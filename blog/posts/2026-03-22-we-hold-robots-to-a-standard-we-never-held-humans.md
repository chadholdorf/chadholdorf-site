A cat got hit by a Waymo in San Francisco. The city nearly banned the entire fleet.

Forty thousand people die in car accidents in the United States every year. Most of those accidents involve a human who was distracted, drunk, tired, or just bad at driving. We don't hold a city-wide emergency over that. We put up a sign.

The asymmetry here isn't a safety standard. It's a double standard.

I get why it exists emotionally. Autonomous vehicles are new, they're run by big tech companies people already distrust, and when something goes wrong it feels like a choice someone made — a design decision, a line of code, a corner cut. Human error feels like weather. Machine error feels like negligence. That's a cognitive bias, not a risk assessment.

But we're letting that bias drive actual policy, and it has real costs.

I've watched this play out in how teams respond to AI output failures. We ship a feature, an edge case surfaces, and suddenly there's a push to roll back the whole thing — even when the same edge case would have been handled worse by the manual process it replaced. The question rarely gets asked: compared to what? Compared to the alternative, not compared to perfection.

The political economy around autonomous vehicles is the same problem at city scale. Politicians in Boston, New York, Seattle — cities where humans are actively terrible at driving — have found that any small slip by a robotaxi is a usable wedge. One incident becomes a news cycle. One news cycle becomes a hearing. One hearing becomes a moratorium. Meanwhile the humans keep doing what humans do on the road, and that's just Tuesday.

If a Cruise-level incident can set back an entire industry by 18 months, we've effectively set the bar at "never make a mistake." No human driver clears that bar. No human driver is expected to.

What would actually make sense: evaluate autonomous vehicles on fleet-wide safety statistics, not individual incidents. The question isn't "did this vehicle make a mistake?" It's "does this fleet, over millions of miles, produce fewer deaths and injuries than human drivers over the same miles?" If yes, the policy answer is obvious.

We don't have to wait for robot Jesus to get behind the wheel. We just have to be honest about what we're comparing it to.

The technology is going to keep improving regardless of whether local politicians feel good about it. The only question is whether we let fear of the new protect us from something that's already much worse.
